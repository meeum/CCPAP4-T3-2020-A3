Here's One of The Things I Never Told You.

Jemma Hollway is throwing herself a pity party, and this time everyone’s invited.

The first time I drove by myself was the night before my year 12 oral presentation. Worth almost half of our marks for english, I was at home frantically writing up cue cards when my Dad called out to me from his bedroom. His face was swollen, coloured a shade of grey I didn’t think skin could look, and tremors ran up and down his body like ocean waves cascading into harsh stone. He was too proud to call an ambulance- Dad had been this way ever since I could remember- and so I loaded him first into the wheelchair we’d acquired from the op shop for emergencies (complete with flat wheels that made it almost impossible to push), packed a backpack full of odd socks and mismatched clothing, and managed to support his worsening state down our front stairs and into the passenger seat of my brand new car.

The darkness of 11pm engulfed both me and my L plates on the trip to the hospital. I’d barely driven at night before, and I remember steering with one hand as I dug my nails into Dad’s arm to keep him awake enough to tell me where to go. When we arrived they slapped a red band on his wrist and took him away from us. My little brother Bailey and I split a stale packet of trail mix from the bottom of my handbag as Mum tried to wrangle herself an earlier flight home from a business trip to come and pick us up. When we finally pulled into our driveway in the very early hours of the next day, we cleaned the piss off my new car seat covers and then ate McDonalds cross legged in the living room watching Finding Nemo. I remember thinking they were the best fries I’d ever had. I guess there’s just something about slightly soggy potato that fixes everything, even when just a few hours ago I'd been sure Dad was going to die in the passenger seat of a car he’d never get the chance to teach me how to reverse park in.

The next day at school when I stuck my head in my locker, sobbing and struggling to breathe everyone thought it was because I’d been too unorganised to prepare my presentation. ‘Classic Jemma’, they would have thought, as if I hadn’t had bigger fish to fry that night than sticking fucking cue cards to coloured squares of craft paper. Go figure.

Multiple Sclerosis is a neurodegenerative disease which effects the central nervous system, and interferes with the nerve impulses within the brain, spinal cord and optic nerves. Dad’s had it for roughly ten years or so, and every step of the way has been a struggle (quite literally). We’ve had surprise trips to the ICU during family holidays, falls at the supermarket, adverse reactions to medications that have left me with a Michelin Man-esque figure in place of a father (okay, this one was kinda funny), as well as an array of bonus complications such as a deaf left ear, an overactive thyroid and a weird eye condition that looks like something you’d either get from a lifelong meth addiction or going swimming in a freshly shat in spa. I know I haven’t exactly painted the prettiest image, but I guess that’s the point isn’t it. There’s nothing exactly glamorous about being sick.

There’s nothing glamorous about pissing yourself in a disability scooter at the local footy, not from being drunk but because your body is failing you and there’s nothing you can do about it. I once had a lady tell me that I needed to try harder in preserving my dad’s independence. I swallowed my pride and thanked her for her unwarranted words of wisdom. After all, how could she possibly have known that I’d already seen the last of it disintegrate that Saturday afternoon when I’d rode in the backseat to pick him up. I couldn’t sleep that night. It’s not often you witness a man lose the very last shred of his dignity.

I’ve never been the type to openly share this with anyone. It’s not like I keep it a secret or anything, I’m not ashamed of my dad or his illness, I’m just not really the ‘sharing personal hardships’ type of person. I am the ‘make a joke of everything kind of girl’. The first time I brought a boy home I said at least we didn’t have to worry about my dad ‘walking’ in on us (as you can imagine that didn’t go down the way I’d hoped). I am the ‘cry in the toilet cubicle while continuously flushing so nobody ever hears you’ kind of person. I may waste a lot of water and take an embarrassingly long time to ‘pee’, but at least no-one will ever look at me like I’ve got a chip on my shoulder (or am in any way, shape, or form dehydrated).

I pride myself on my independence, and on that front I can thank my mum. When I was little I’d sit on her bed and listen to her singing in the shower. Sometimes she’d talk to herself instead, asking questions aloud and working through the solutions calm and logically, other times with a string of swear words attached and followed by an angry rendition of whichever Journey song she liked at the time. I’d always wondered why she didn’t just ask the questions to someone else, to me that option sounded like it would save an awful lot of time and heartache.

I didn’t realise until I was older that the problem (my epiphany came last week as I stressed over the impending reality of a full time carer for Dad whilst simultaneously toning my hair with purple shampoo) was that half the time we didn’t even know how to ask for help. I’d put so much emphasis on being independent that sharing my problems with someone else besides my shower head presented itself as a completely terrifying, yet invigorating idea.

To tell you the truth, I’ve decided that I’m getting kind of sick of trying not to have a chip on my shoulder. There are so many people in the world that have it worse than me, so many that sometimes it feels like I don’t deserve to be sad about anything, and maybe I don’t. I just think that sometimes it would be nice for someone to see me sobbing into my locker, and know that it’s because I’m scared and overwhelmed and frustrated, rather than assuming it’s because I haven’t done my homework (which in all honesty, I probably haven’t either).

I just want a hug, and someone to tell me it’s going to work out okay. It’s little things like that you simply can’t do by yourself, and I’m beginning to realise they’re the things I so desperately need.

Okay so maybe I’m not having a full blown pity party after all, but surely there’s no harm in letting someone shout me a few sympathy ciders here and there. After all, I reckon I fucking deserve it.
