The Soaring Trajectory of Adrian Eagle.

Adrian Eagle remembers the exact moment his debut EP MAMA went public. The multi-award winning singer-songwriter from Adelaide had been hanging backstage at the Corner Hotel with fellow Triple-J Unearthed success Toni Watson (Tonnes and I) after their second show of her inaugural National tour at the end of last year. High on adrenaline and surrounded by a close knit group of crew and loved ones, when the clock finally struck midnight on the 19th of September he’d broken down in tears, overcome with a lifetime’s worth of emotion. “It was just that crazy feeling,” he tells me, almost exactly 8 months later and sporting a contagious smile that’s visible even over the phone. “Just the feeling of knowing that once it’s out for the people, it’s out there forever, and for as long as Earth exists… It’s artwork from my heart and it’s out there."

Bold, self-assured and remarkably cognisant, it’s hard to image Adrian any differently. Throughout our conversation he speaks with conviction, never once faltering in his message. When I stutter or stumble over the question in front of me he’s patient, answering with kindness and warmth.

Words, he says, have the capacity to change the world—and while the journey to this discovery hasn’t been an easy one by any means, he’s doing everything he can to make up for lost time.

“Music is so influential you know…” he proclaims, when we finally sit down to chat on Monday morning. “Humans are so influential. I’ve been victim to evil music before where I would just listen to this sort of toxic undertone in a lot of lyrics.”

“Sometimes the more positive and loving lyrics are deemed as corny or not cool, so I guess what I was definitely trying to do was make music that could be soulful, and sort of lift the spirit as well as lift my own spirit.”

It’s 2020, and anger has never been more on-trend. We’re a generation that seems to pride ourselves on either our rash and often dramatic hatred (cue cancel culture and twitter feminism) or worse yet, the ability to appear completely apathetic— painting ourselves out as perfect connect the dots replicas of ‘too cool to care’.

Whether it’s in the music we listen to, the ideas we romanticise or the people we envy, most of us have probably fallen victim to hate’s sturdy grip at some stage. For Adrian, it’s not so much learning to stop these feelings, but about channelling them into something good instead.

“Music has that platform to literally put words into the community,” he gushes, excitedly. “To put chants, and mantras into the community, especially when it’s going on radio or out to new people. It’s such a high level of importance to me. It’s a fun process as well, and I definitely feel how it can make a change— even if it’s just one person’s life.”

‘A.O.K’ stands for Acts of Kindness, and is not only the third track on last year’s six song collection— but an uplifting reminder of the responsibility you have for the space around you. It’s an anthem of resilience, love, and most importantly, the power of positivity. From a rising hip-hop artist, the revelation is refreshingly peculiar.

Although Adrian admits “there’s people out there who might hear ‘A.O.K’ and think ‘shut this shit off man!’” He says he completely understands. “I have experienced the jealousy, the negativity, that sort of not understanding my sound or that kind of vibe but it’s okay. It’s alright, because it’s such a huge world.”

“What I’ve realised is, the people at my shows are very much like me, as in they’ve maybe been through something traumatic in their life, or have just been through something where they’ve had enough of it and they’re not trying to settle for that type of environment— not trying to settle for that type of brainwashing or that type of negativity anymore.”

Adrian was just seventeen years old when he stopped trying to kill himself. It’s a line he sings in his break-out hit ‘17 Again’, and something that’s managed to resonate with not only himself but the thousands of people who now listen to it everyday. It depicts one of the toughest periods of his life, encapsulating the story of his struggle with both physical and mental health, but also the clear message of his music overall. Hold on, things get better.

“A lot of the time it’s other people’s negativity that can bring us down. Other people’s nastiness, or meanness and in-compassionateness in themselves, so it takes the other side to balance us out a bit,” he explains.

“I guess if you tune into my music, you’re going through the same emotions I’ve been through, and you’re looking for that positive reaffirmation that life is a journey, and to keep on going because everything is possible as far as you know. My life is that story… and that’s the reality, to never give up.”

Adrian’s working hard on some pretty big stuff right now (“man, my tail is wagging just thinking about it. I’m so happy!”) and although we can’t yet disclose any specific dates, keep a keen eye out on his Spotify and socials for updates. Until then, check out MAMA here.
